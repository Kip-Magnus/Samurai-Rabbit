# SAMURAI USAGI — THE PLOT BIBLE
## *A Time-Traveling Saint's Chronicle Through 2000 Years of Church History*

---

## I. CORE CONCEPT

**Logline:** A humble rabbit farmer in feudal Japan is called by heaven to become a wandering saint — pulled across time and space to the great crisis moments of Church history, answering the prayers of the faithful who cry out for intercession, wielding a blade in one paw and a rosary in the other.

**The Hook:** Usagi is not a hero who chose adventure. He is a simple creature who said "yes" to God — and that "yes" echoes across two thousand years. Like the saints before him, his power is not his own. He is an instrument. Every battle, every miracle, every moment of doubt is a question posed to the player: *What does it cost to be good in a world that punishes goodness?*

**Genre DNA:** The game draws from Chrono Trigger's time-travel structure (multiple eras, each a self-contained world with connecting threads), Final Fantasy Tactics' political intrigue and moral ambiguity, Dragon Quest V's life-spanning emotional arc, and Tactics Ogre's factional warfare where no side is purely righteous. But unlike those games, the moral compass here is fixed: virtue is real, grace is real, and evil — while seductive — is always a lie.

---

## II. NARRATIVE ARCHITECTURE

### The Three-Layer Structure

Every great RPG story operates on three simultaneous layers:

**Layer 1 — The Personal Journey (Usagi's soul)**
Who is this rabbit? What does he fear? What tempts him? His arc moves from reluctant obedience → tested faith → dark night of the soul → surrender to providence → sanctification. He is not perfect. He doubts. He rages. He weeps. But he keeps getting back up. This is what makes a saint — not perfection, but perseverance.

**Layer 2 — The Historical Crisis (the era)**
Each time period is a fully realized world with its own politics, factions, betrayals, and stakes. The player must navigate real theological and military conflicts. The crusaders at Acre are not cartoon heroes — some are holy, some are venal. The Japanese daimyo who persecutes Christians is not a mustache-twirling villain — he genuinely believes he is protecting his nation. Moral complexity makes the player *think*.

**Layer 3 — The Cosmic War (heaven vs. hell)**
Behind every historical crisis, unseen forces are at work. Demons whisper in the ears of kings. Angels hold back destruction for one more hour. Heresies are not merely intellectual errors — they are spiritual weapons designed to fracture the Body of Christ. Usagi, with his gift of spiritual sight, can perceive both layers: the political and the supernatural. This dual vision is what makes him indispensable.

### The Expandable Episode Structure

Each major era is a self-contained "Book" — a complete story with its own beginning, middle, and end. But threads connect across Books: recurring angelic guides, demonic adversaries who reappear in new guises, artifacts that show up centuries apart, and the slow revelation of *why* Usagi was chosen.

This structure means the game can grow endlessly. Each new Book is a new era, a new crisis, a new set of characters — but always connected to the whole.

## III. THE ORIGIN — BOOK ZERO: THE CALLING

### Setting: Feudal Japan, 1587

The Shimabara Peninsula. Christianity has been flourishing in Japan for forty years since Francis Xavier arrived in 1549. Tens of thousands of Japanese have converted. But Toyotomi Hideyoshi has just issued his edict banning Jesuit missionaries. The political winds are turning.

### The Story

Usagi is a farmer-rabbit in a small village on the outskirts of Nagasaki. He is no warrior. He tends his rice paddies. He attends Mass in a hidden chapel with other *kakure kirishitan* — hidden Christians. His grandmother, who raised him after his parents died in a flood, taught him the faith. She gave him a small wooden cross and told him: *"When the world is dark, hold this and pray. Heaven always answers — but not always the way you expect."*

One evening, Usagi's village is raided by samurai enforcing Hideyoshi's ban. The hidden chapel is burned. His grandmother, rather than step on the *fumi-e* (an image of Christ used to test Christians), refuses. She is martyred before his eyes.

Usagi flees into the mountains clutching her cross. In his grief and rage, he cries out to God — not a prayer of faith but a scream of accusation: *"Where were You?"*

And heaven answers.

A light splits the darkness. Not an angel in the traditional sense — something stranger. A figure who identifies himself only as **The Messenger** appears: a tall, luminous being who speaks in riddles and Scripture. He tells Usagi that his grandmother's prayer — her final prayer, offered with her dying breath — was heard. That prayer was not for herself. It was for the world. And Usagi is the answer.

The Messenger gives Usagi two gifts:
- **The Blade of the Pilgrim** — a simple katana that grows in power as Usagi's faith deepens
- **The Charism of Passage** — the ability to be pulled across time to wherever the faithful cry out for intercession

Usagi doesn't understand. He doesn't want this. But The Messenger says: *"You are not asked to understand. You are asked to go."*

And the first portal opens.

## IV. THE BOOKS — CRISIS ERAS

Each Book follows this rhythm:
1. **Arrival** — Usagi is pulled to a new era, disoriented, alone
2. **Immersion** — He must learn the local crisis, find allies, earn trust
3. **Complication** — The political situation is more tangled than it appears; a heresy or spiritual corruption is making things worse
4. **The Dark Moment** — Usagi faces a moral impossible choice, or a defeat that tests his faith
5. **Intercession** — Through prayer, sacrifice, or a miracle, the tide turns
6. **Resolution** — The crisis is resolved (not always happily), and Usagi is pulled to the next era

---

### BOOK I: THE ARENA (Rome, 64 AD)

**Historical Basis:** The Neronian Persecution — Nero blames Christians for the Great Fire of Rome. Christians are wrapped in animal skins and torn apart by dogs, burned as human torches, crucified. Peter and Paul are both in Rome.

**The Crisis:** Usagi arrives in the catacombs beneath Rome. The Christian community is in hiding. Some want to flee. Some want to fight. Some want to deny their faith to survive. A charismatic figure named **Marcellus** is urging the community to take up arms against Rome, arguing that God helps those who help themselves.

**The Theological Conflict — Early Gnosticism:** Among the Roman Christians, a teacher named **Demas** is spreading a proto-Gnostic doctrine: that the material world is evil, that the body doesn't matter, and therefore it doesn't matter if you step on the *fumi-e* equivalent (offering incense to the emperor) because only the spirit counts. "Your body is a prison," Demas teaches. "What you do with it is irrelevant to your soul." This is seductive to terrified people looking for permission to save their own lives.

**Usagi's Role:** He must protect the faithful being hunted for the arena, while navigating between Marcellus (who wants holy war) and Demas (who wants comfortable apostasy). Neither is right. The true path — the one Peter himself walks — is martyrdom accepted freely. Not suicide. Not violence. But witness.

**The Dark Moment:** Usagi is captured and thrown into the Colosseum. He must fight — not Romans, but beasts. His faith is tested: does he use his supernatural gifts to escape (which would expose the hidden Christians), or does he trust God in the arena?

**Resolution:** Through a miracle of intercession — perhaps a bilocation that allows him to be in the arena AND in the catacombs warning the faithful — Usagi saves lives without betraying the community. Peter, about to be crucified upside down, gives Usagi a blessing that echoes across every future Book.

---

### BOOK II: THE DESERT FATHERS (Egypt, 325 AD)

**Historical Basis:** The Arian Crisis — Arius of Alexandria teaches that Christ is a created being, not co-eternal with the Father. The Council of Nicaea is assembling. Meanwhile, the Desert Fathers (Anthony, Pachomius) are establishing monasticism in the Egyptian wilderness.

**The Crisis:** The Arian heresy is tearing the Church apart. Bishops are excommunicating each other. Emperor Constantine wants unity but doesn't care about theology — he just wants peace. A powerful Arian bishop named **Secundus** is using imperial connections to have orthodox bishops exiled or killed.

**The Theological Conflict — Arianism:** "Christ is the greatest of created beings — the firstborn of all creation — but He is not God Himself." This sounds reasonable. It sounds humble. And it is devastatingly wrong, because if Christ is not God, then God did not enter human suffering, and redemption is a lie. Usagi must grasp why this "small" theological distinction matters more than any battle.

**Usagi's Role:** He arrives in the desert and finds refuge with the monks. But even the monastery is divided — some monks are Arian, some orthodox. Usagi must journey to Nicaea carrying a message from Athanasius (the great defender of orthodoxy), navigating imperial politics, Arian assassins, and his own doubts.

**Companion Character — Brother Pachomius:** A gentle monk with the gift of discernment of spirits who can sense demonic influence. He teaches Usagi that the Arian heresy is not merely an intellectual error — it is inspired by a spiritual force that wants to diminish Christ.

**The Dark Moment:** Usagi discovers that Secundus has made a pact with a demonic entity — not through occult ritual, but through pride. The demon didn't need to appear in smoke and fire. It simply whispered: *"You are right. They are wrong. And you deserve to win."* The most dangerous possession is the one that feels like conviction.

---

### BOOK III: THE SIEGE (Jerusalem, 1099 / Acre, 1291)

**Historical Basis:** The First Crusade's capture of Jerusalem and/or the Fall of Acre — the last Crusader stronghold.

**The Crisis:** This Book confronts the Crusades honestly. Usagi arrives among Crusaders who are a mixture of genuine pilgrims, cynical mercenaries, fanatical zealots, and saints. The commander, a knight named **Godfrey**, truly believes he is serving God. His lieutenant, **Reynald**, is a butcher who uses religion to justify plunder.

**The Theological Conflict — Just War vs. Holy War:** When does defense of the faith become aggression? When does righteous anger become bloodlust? Augustine's just war doctrine is debated by the Crusaders' chaplain, **Father Bernard**, who argues for restraint — but Reynald mocks him as a coward.

**Usagi's Role:** He must protect innocent civilians (Christian, Muslim, and Jewish) caught in the siege while fighting alongside the genuine defenders of pilgrims. This Book forces the player to confront the moral complexity of the Crusades — neither whitewashing them nor condemning them wholesale.

**Key Scene:** Usagi encounters **Francis of Assisi** (if set during the Fifth Crusade, 1219) crossing enemy lines to speak with Sultan al-Kamil. Francis walks unarmed into the enemy camp. Usagi, sword in hand, must decide: does he follow the way of the sword or the way of the cross? The answer is both — but knowing when to use which is the wisdom of saints.

---

### BOOK IV: THE MONGOL STORM (Poland/Hungary, 1241)

**Historical Basis:** The Mongol invasion of Europe — the Battle of Liegnitz where Duke Henry II of Silesia and the Knights Templar faced the Mongol horde. Europe's darkest hour — if the Mongols had pressed on, Christendom might have fallen.

**The Crisis:** The Mongols are unstoppable. They have shattered every army sent against them. Poland is burning. Hungary is falling. The pope has called a crusade against the Mongols, but the German host has dispersed due to political infighting. Europe is fractured — the Emperor and the Pope are feuding (the Investiture Controversy's echoes), and no united defense is possible.

**The Theological Conflict — Despair and Providence:** When God seems absent from history, when evil seems to be winning on every front, how do you maintain faith? The temptation here is not a heresy of doctrine but a heresy of the heart: **acedia** — spiritual sloth, the death of hope. A monk named **Brother Casimir** has stopped praying. "God has abandoned Poland," he says. "Why pray to an empty sky?"

**Usagi's Role:** He fights alongside the Templars at Liegnitz — a battle that is historically a defeat. Usagi cannot change history (this is a crucial rule of the game's time-travel), but he can change *hearts*. His mission is not to win the battle but to ensure that the faith survives the defeat. He must inspire Brother Casimir to pray again. Because historically, the Mongols withdrew after Liegnitz — not because of military defeat, but because the Great Khan Ögödei died, and the generals returned home for the succession. Providence works through strange doors.

**Key Moment:** The night before the battle, Usagi prays the Rosary with a handful of Polish peasant-soldiers. None of them will survive tomorrow. But they pray anyway. This is the mystery — faith does not promise survival. It promises meaning.

---

### BOOK V: THE HIDDEN CHURCH (Japan, 1614-1638)

**Historical Basis:** The Tokugawa persecution of Christians — the ban of 1614, the martyrdom of the 26 Martyrs of Japan, the Shimabara Rebellion.

**The Crisis:** Usagi returns to Japan — but centuries after his origin. Christianity is now outlawed. The *kakure kirishitan* worship in secret. The *fumi-e* is used to identify and execute Christians. The Shimabara Rebellion is brewing — Christian peasants pushed to breaking point by taxation and persecution.

**The Theological Conflict — Apostasy and Mercy:** This is the question posed by Shusaku Endo's novel *Silence*: Is it ever acceptable to apostatize to save others? A Jesuit priest named **Father Rodrigues** (an homage) is captured and told that if he steps on the *fumi-e*, the imprisoned Christians will be freed. If he refuses, they will be tortured to death. What does faithfulness look like when your faithfulness causes the suffering of others?

**Usagi's Personal Crisis:** This is HIS homeland. These are HIS people. He recognizes the descendants of his village. He finds his grandmother's cross — passed down through generations. He is the saint his own grandmother prayed for, sent across centuries to answer her prayer. The emotional weight of this revelation is the heart of the game.

**The Dark Moment:** Usagi must choose whether to support the Shimabara rebels (a violent uprising that will end in massacre) or to counsel patience and hidden faith (which means more years of suffering). There is no clean answer. This is the Book where Usagi's faith cracks — and through the cracks, something deeper enters.

---

### BOOK VI: THE SEA OF FIRE (Lepanto, 1571)

**Historical Basis:** The Battle of Lepanto — the Holy League's naval victory over the Ottoman fleet, credited to the intercession of Our Lady of the Rosary.

**The Crisis:** The Ottoman Empire is at the height of its power. Cyprus has fallen. The Mediterranean is an Ottoman lake. Pope Pius V has assembled a Holy League, but it is fractured: Venice wants trade, Spain wants glory, and the knights want honor. Don Juan of Austria, the young commander, must hold them together.

**The Theological Conflict — Protestantism and Christendom's Fracture:** England's Protestant queen refuses to help. France, wracked by the Wars of Religion between Catholics and Huguenots, sits out. The Reformation has shattered the unity of Christendom, and the Ottoman threat exploits every crack. A Protestant English merchant named **Thomas Hale**, shipwrecked and rescued by the Holy League, challenges Usagi: "Your pope sells indulgences and calls it salvation. My Bible says faith alone saves. Why should I fight for Rome?" Usagi must answer — not with a sword, but with the truth that transcends denominational boundaries.

**Usagi's Role:** He fights in the galley ships, but his deeper mission is spiritual. Pope Pius V has asked all of Christendom to pray the Rosary for victory. Usagi discovers that the battle is being fought on two planes — the physical sea and the spiritual realm. In the spirit, he can see demonic forces empowering the Ottoman fleet and angelic hosts responding to the prayers of the faithful across Europe.

**Key Scene:** As the battle rages, Usagi perceives a vision: thousands of people across Europe, in churches and homes, praying the Rosary simultaneously. Each prayer is a point of light. The lights form a chain. The chain becomes a shield. This is the Communion of Saints made visible — the idea that prayer has real, material power.

---

### BOOK VII: THE TERROR (France, 1793)

**Historical Basis:** The French Revolution's Reign of Terror — the dechristianization campaign, the execution of the Carmelite Martyrs of Compiègne, the desecration of churches.

**The Crisis:** The Revolution has turned from political reform to spiritual annihilation. Churches are converted to "Temples of Reason." Priests are forced to swear loyalty to the state or face the guillotine. The Carmelite nuns of Compiègne are arrested for refusing to abandon their vows.

**The Theological Conflict — Enlightenment Rationalism and Pelagianism Reborn:** The revolutionaries embody a modern form of Pelagianism: the belief that human reason and will alone can perfect humanity without God's grace. Robespierre's "Cult of the Supreme Being" is not atheism — it is worse. It is the attempt to keep God's name while emptying it of everything that makes God, God. A revolutionary named **Citizen Danton** (fictional) tells Usagi: "We have no need of your miracles. Man is his own salvation." This is Pelagius in a Jacobin cap.

**Usagi's Role:** He must protect the Carmelite nuns as they are taken to the guillotine. Historically, the sixteen nuns sang the *Veni Creator Spiritus* as they climbed the scaffold, each one renewing her baptismal vows before the blade fell. Usagi cannot save them from death — but he can ensure that their sacrifice is not in vain.

**The Dark Moment:** Usagi is offered a deal by a demon masquerading as a revolutionary official: "Deny your faith publicly, and I will spare the nuns." It is the *fumi-e* again — the same test, in a different century. Usagi realizes that this test follows him through time because it is THE test — the one question the Enemy always asks: *"Will you trade eternity for comfort?"*

---

### BOOK VIII: THE UNDERGROUND (Poland, 1940s)

**Historical Basis:** The Nazi occupation of Poland — the persecution of the Catholic Church, Maximilian Kolbe's sacrifice at Auschwitz, the Warsaw Uprising.

**The Crisis:** 3,000 Polish clergy will be killed by the Nazis. Churches are desecrated. Catholic intellectuals are executed. In Auschwitz, a Franciscan friar named Maximilian Kolbe volunteers to die in place of a stranger.

**The Theological Conflict — Nihilism, Predestination, and the Problem of Evil:** This is where Calvinism's shadow falls over the narrative — not through Protestant characters specifically, but through the question that Calvinist theology raises in its most extreme form: If God is sovereign over all things, did He *will* Auschwitz? A prisoner named **Jakob**, a Calvinist pastor arrested for sheltering Jews, puts it to Usagi bluntly: "If your God is all-powerful and all-good, explain this camp. Either He cannot stop it, or He will not. Which is it?" Usagi has no easy answer. The Catholic response — that God permits evil but does not will it, that free will is real, that suffering can be redemptive — is intellectually coherent but emotionally devastating when spoken in a death camp.

**Usagi's Role:** He works alongside the resistance — smuggling food, carrying messages, protecting the vulnerable. His final mission is to be present at Kolbe's sacrifice: the moment when a man freely chooses to die for a stranger. This is the answer to Jakob's question — not an argument but a witness. God's response to evil is not explanation but incarnation. He enters the suffering.

**Key Scene:** Kolbe, starving in the death bunker, leads the condemned prisoners in hymns. Usagi, hidden among them through bilocation, holds Kolbe's hand as he dies. In that moment, Usagi understands his own calling fully: he is not sent to *prevent* suffering. He is sent to *be present* in it. That is what the saints have always done.

## V. THE RECURRING ANTAGONIST — THE ACCUSER

Across all Books, a single demonic intelligence operates: **The Accuser** (Ha-Satan in the original Hebrew sense — "the prosecutor"). He does not appear as a horned monster. He appears as:

- A reasonable Roman senator in Book I
- A brilliant Arian theologian in Book II
- A persuasive Crusader chaplain in Book III who argues for total war
- A Mongol shaman in Book IV
- A sympathetic Japanese magistrate in Book V who "just wants peace"
- A Protestant pamphleteer in Book VI who sows division
- A Jacobin philosopher in Book VII
- A camp commandant in Book VIII who quotes Nietzsche

He is always the same being. He always makes the same offer: *"You can end the suffering. Just compromise. Just a little. What is one small betrayal against so much good you could do?"*

Usagi learns to recognize him — not by his face, which changes, but by his argument, which never does. The Accuser never asks you to do something obviously evil. He asks you to do something *almost* good, for *almost* the right reason, in a way that *almost* doesn't cost you anything. That is the nature of real temptation.

## VI. THE THEOLOGICAL HERESIES AS GAME MECHANICS

Each heresy Usagi encounters is not just a plot point — it manifests as a gameplay threat:

| Heresy | Era | Core Error | Game Manifestation |
|--------|-----|-----------|-------------------|
| **Gnosticism** | Rome, 64 AD | "Matter is evil; the body doesn't matter" | Enemies who phase through physical attacks; can only be harmed by holy skills. NPCs who offer power by "transcending the flesh" (stat boosts with hidden HP drain) |
| **Arianism** | Egypt, 325 AD | "Christ is created, not God" | Holy skills are weakened in Arian-corrupted zones; Usagi must "restore orthodoxy" to altars to regain full power |
| **Donatism** | (Background, multiple eras) | "Sacraments from sinful priests are invalid" | Companion NPCs who refuse to fight alongside "impure" allies, fracturing your party at critical moments |
| **Pelagianism** | France, 1793 (and recurring) | "Man can save himself without grace" | A status effect: "Self-Reliance" — boosts ATK but blocks all healing and prayer skills. Seductive but fatal |
| **Iconoclasm** | (Side quest) | "Religious images are sinful" | Destroying sacred icons weakens the party's spiritual protection; protecting them strengthens it |
| **Calvinism/Predestination** | Poland, 1940s | "God wills all that happens, including evil" | Despair mechanic — characters who succumb believe their actions don't matter, reducing all stats. Usagi must actively counter with acts of free will |
| **Protestantism (Sola Fide)** | Lepanto, 1571 | "Faith alone, works are irrelevant" | Dialogue choices: if Usagi argues that faith AND works matter, Protestant allies may leave — but the party's spiritual power increases. Finding common ground keeps the party whole |

## VII. THE VIRTUE SYSTEM

Usagi is a virtue-bound warrior. The game tracks seven virtues, each tied to a saint patron:

| Virtue | Patron Saint | Effect | Tested By |
|--------|-------------|--------|-----------|
| **Prudence** | St. Thomas Aquinas | Better dialogue options, reveals hidden paths | Situations requiring restraint when action feels urgent |
| **Justice** | St. Louis IX | Increased damage against oppressors, better merchant prices | Temptations to take shortcuts or accept bribes |
| **Fortitude** | St. Joan of Arc | Resistance to fear/despair effects, bonus HP in dire fights | Moments of overwhelming odds, torture, capture |
| **Temperance** | St. Benedict | Resistance to status effects (poison, rage), MP conservation | Offers of excess — power, wealth, vengeance |
| **Faith** | St. Peter | Holy skill power, miracle chance | Moments when God seems absent — unanswered prayers, innocent suffering |
| **Hope** | St. Maximilian Kolbe | Party morale, prevents despair debuff on companions | Extended suffering, imprisonment, loss of companions |
| **Charity** | St. Martin de Porres | Healing power, NPC trust, hidden quest access | Choices between self-preservation and helping others |

Virtues increase through *actions*, not XP. Giving your last potion to a wounded NPC raises Charity. Standing your ground when retreat is logical raises Fortitude. The player is never told which actions raise which virtues — they discover it naturally through play.

Virtues can also *decrease*. Hoarding items lowers Charity. Running from a fight you should face lowers Fortitude. Taking revenge lowers Justice. The system is forgiving but real.

## VIII. COMPANION ARCHETYPES

Each Book introduces 1-2 companions who join Usagi's party. They represent different perspectives on faith and war:

**The Believer** — A character of deep, simple faith. Often a peasant, a nun, a child. They remind Usagi (and the player) why the fight matters. They are vulnerable. Protecting them is not a burden — it is the point.

**The Doubter** — A character who challenges Usagi's faith with hard questions. They are never stupid or evil. Their doubts are real. The Protestant merchant, the Calvinist pastor, the exhausted monk who stopped praying — these characters force the narrative to earn its theology instead of preaching it.

**The Warrior** — A fighter whose loyalty is to honor or nation rather than faith. A Japanese ronin, a Templar knight, a Polish cavalry officer. They share Usagi's commitment to protecting the weak but disagree about *why*. Their arc is often about discovering that virtue without transcendence is fragile.

**The Convert** — A character who begins as an enemy or outsider and comes to faith through Usagi's witness. Not through argument — through seeing someone live what they claim to believe. The most powerful evangelism in the game is never verbal. It is behavioral.

## IX. THE RULES OF TIME TRAVEL

1. **Usagi cannot change major historical events.** The Fall of Acre falls. The Carmelites die. Kolbe dies. History is inviolable. What Usagi changes is the *spiritual* outcome — the souls saved, the faith preserved, the witness given.

2. **Usagi is pulled, never chooses.** He does not control the Charism of Passage. He is sent where he is needed. This prevents the "why didn't you just go back and fix it" problem — he is an instrument, not an architect.

3. **Time is not linear for the spiritual realm.** The Accuser remembers Usagi from previous encounters even though they are centuries apart. Angels likewise operate outside time. The Messenger appears in every era, always enigmatic, always knowing more than he says.

4. **Usagi ages, but slowly.** The charisms sustain him, but each journey takes a toll. By the later Books, he is visibly older, scarred, weary. This is not a power fantasy — it is a pilgrimage, and pilgrimages cost.

5. **Artifacts persist across time.** His grandmother's cross, the Blade of the Pilgrim, relics gifted by saints — these travel with him. Some items found in early eras appear again later, their history enriched.

## X. FUTURE BOOKS (EXPANSION ROADMAP)

The structure allows infinite expansion. Some potential future eras:

- **Carthage, 250 AD** — The Decian Persecution; Cyprian's debate over readmitting the lapsed (Donatism's roots)
- **Tours, 732 AD** — Charles Martel halts the Islamic advance into Europe
- **Constantinople, 1453** — The Fall of the Eastern Roman Empire; the Great Schism's consequences
- **Mexico, 1531** — Our Lady of Guadalupe; the conversion of millions; the question of inculturation
- **England, 1535** — Thomas More's execution; Henry VIII's break with Rome; the birth of Anglicanism
- **Vienna, 1683** — The Ottoman siege broken by Jan III Sobieski and the winged hussars
- **Vendée, 1793** — The Catholic peasant uprising against the French Revolution
- **Cristero War, Mexico, 1926** — "Viva Cristo Rey!" — the Catholic resistance to anti-clerical government
- **China, 1950s** — The underground Catholic Church under Mao; Cardinal Kung's imprisonment
- **The Present Day** — Usagi arrives in the modern world, confronting relativism, materialism, and the culture of indifference — the final and perhaps most dangerous heresy: not opposition to God, but apathy toward Him

## XI. THEMATIC PILLARS — THE SOUL OF THE GAME

### 1. "The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the Church" — Tertullian
Suffering is not meaningless. Every sacrifice in this game has consequences — not always visible, not always immediate, but real. The player who protects the weak, even at great cost, will find that cost repaid in unexpected ways: a descendant of someone you saved appears in a later era as an ally. A prayer offered in one century is answered in another.

### 2. "Be not afraid" — John Paul II
Fear is the primary weapon of the Enemy. Not violence — fear of violence. Not suffering — fear of suffering. Every Book presents Usagi with something terrifying, and every Book asks the same question: Will you go forward anyway?

### 3. "In the world you will have tribulation. But take heart; I have overcome the world." — John 16:33
The game does not promise happy endings. Some Books end in historical tragedy. But it promises that no act of love is wasted, no prayer is unheard, and no darkness is final. The final Book — whenever it is written — will end with Usagi, old and broken and luminous with grace, entering the presence of God. Not because he was strong. Because he was faithful.

### 4. Virtue is not the absence of temptation — it is the presence of love
The game never makes goodness look easy. It makes it look costly and beautiful. The player should finish each Book thinking not "I won" but "that mattered." The greatest RPGs make you feel something true. This one aims to make you feel that being good — truly, sacrificially, stubbornly good — is the most heroic thing a person can do.

*"Greater love has no one than this: to lay down one's life for one's friends." — John 15:13*

*This is the story of a rabbit who did exactly that — again, and again, and again, across two thousand years.*
