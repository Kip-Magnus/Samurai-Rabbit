# BOOK ZERO: THE CALLING
## *Complete Narrative Design Document*

---

## SETTING: THE SHIMABARA PENINSULA, 1587

### The World

It is late summer. The rice paddies glow amber in the evening light. Cicadas scream in the cedar forests above the village of **Minamata** — a small farming settlement on the western coast of Kyushu, within sight of Nagasaki harbor. Ships from Portugal and Spain still dock there, carrying silk, gunpowder, clocks — and priests.

Christianity has been in Japan for thirty-eight years. Francis Xavier landed at Kagoshima in 1549. Since then, the Jesuits have converted tens of thousands — entire villages, some daimyo, even samurai clans. Churches have been built. The Mass is said in Latin with Japanese hymns. The faith has taken root in the volcanic soil of Kyushu like a camphor tree — deep, fragrant, and impossible to uproot without violence.

But the violence is coming.

On July 24, 1587, Toyotomi Hideyoshi — the second Great Unifier of Japan, the most powerful man in the country — issued the **Bateren Edict**: all foreign missionaries must leave Japan within twenty days. Christianity is now suspect. The reasons are political as much as spiritual: a Spanish ship captain boasted that Spain conquers nations by sending priests first and soldiers after. Hideyoshi heard this. He will not be conquered.

The edict is not yet enforced with killing. Not yet. Some missionaries leave. Many stay, going underground. The Japanese Christians — the **Kirishitan** — are caught between worlds. Their faith is real. Their danger is real. And neither is going away.

## PROLOGUE: THE VILLAGE

### Scene 1: Morning in Minamata

*[Gameplay: Tutorial area. The player learns basic movement, interaction, and the daily rhythms of village life.]*

The game opens on dawn. Pink light over the sea. A rooster crows. The camera slowly zooms in on a small thatched farmhouse at the edge of a rice paddy, where a rabbit in a roughspun kimono is already awake, kneeling on a woven mat.

**USAGI** is praying.

Not dramatically. Not with visible light or angelic choirs. He is kneeling before a small wooden shelf in the corner of his room — the **kamidana** (household shrine) — but instead of a Shinto talisman, there is a small wooden crucifix hidden behind a ceramic figure of Kannon, the bodhisattva of mercy. This is how the Kirishitan worship: in plain sight, hidden in plain sight.

His grandmother, **OBA-CHAN** (real name: **Magdalena**), calls from the other room: *"Usagi-kun. The rice won't plant itself."*

The player controls Usagi as he goes about morning tasks — drawing water from the well, feeding the chickens, checking the rice paddies. These are not busywork; they establish the *normalcy* that is about to be shattered. The village is peaceful. Neighbors greet each other. Children play. A merchant sells dried fish. An old man repairs a fishing net.

**Design Note:** Every NPC in the village has a name, a personality, and a faith. Some are openly Christian (the Kirishitan are not yet hunted — the edict is fresh and unenforced). Some are Buddhist. Some practice both, as many Japanese did. The player discovers this through conversation, not exposition.

### Key NPCs in Minamata

**OBA-CHAN (MAGDALENA)** — Usagi's grandmother. Age 68. A farmer's widow who was among the first converts in the village, baptized by a Portuguese Jesuit thirty years ago. She is small, fierce, and tender. She prays the Rosary every night using a string of wooden beads she carved herself. She calls God "*Deusu*" — the Japanese rendering of the Portuguese "Deus" that the Kirishitan used. She is the emotional anchor of the entire game. Everything that happens to Usagi flows from her.

**FATHER TOMAS** — A Portuguese Jesuit priest, age 45, who has lived in Japan for twenty years. He speaks fluent Japanese. He is the village's spiritual leader but is now in hiding — he was supposed to leave under the Bateren Edict but chose to stay. He holds secret Mass in a cave outside the village. He is gentle, scholarly, and carries a terrible weight: he knows the persecution will get worse, and he cannot protect his flock.

**KENJI** — Usagi's childhood friend. A Buddhist fisherman's son. He does not share Usagi's faith but respects it. He is brave, reckless, and loyal. He represents the "warrior without faith" archetype — honor without transcendence. He will be Usagi's first companion in combat, and his eventual fate is one of the game's most painful moments.

**HANA** — A young woman who tends the village's silkworms. She is secretly Kirishitan — baptized **Maria** — but her father is a minor samurai loyal to Hideyoshi. She lives in constant tension between her faith and her family. She represents the cost of hidden belief.

**LORD MATSUDA** — The local magistrate. Not evil. Not cruel. A bureaucrat who received orders from his daimyo to investigate Christian activity. He does not want to hurt anyone. He wants to file reports that keep his superiors satisfied while causing minimal disruption. He is the banality of persecution — the man who "just follows orders" without malice but also without courage.

### Scene 2: The Hidden Mass

*[Gameplay: The player follows Father Tomas to a sea cave at dusk. Stealth/navigation tutorial. Introduction to the prayer system.]*

As evening falls, Oba-chan takes Usagi by the hand and leads him along a cliff path to a cave overlooking the sea. Inside, lit by candles made from whale oil, twenty villagers are gathered. Father Tomas celebrates Mass on a flat stone altar.

This scene establishes the **prayer mechanic**. During the Mass, the player participates in a simplified liturgical rhythm — responding to prayers at the right time (a timing minigame, not unlike rhythm games, but solemn and quiet). Success does not grant XP — it grants **Grace**, the game's spiritual currency, which powers miracles and healing.

The scene also introduces **The Orashio** — the Kirishitan oral prayers. These are historical: the Japanese Christians memorized prayers in a pidgin of Latin, Portuguese, and Japanese that became garbled over generations but was fiercely preserved. In the game, these prayers appear as text that the player can collect — fragments of the Ave Maria, the Pater Noster, and the Credo in the Kirishitan tongue:

> *"Deusu no on-tsukuri nasareta ten to chi no on-aruji..."*
> (Lord God who made heaven and earth...)

**Design Note:** The Orashio fragments serve double duty — they are collectibles that flesh out the world's lore, AND they are "spell components" that unlock prayer abilities in the skill tree. Finding all the Orashio in a given Book unlocks a powerful capstone prayer.

After Mass, Father Tomas pulls Usagi aside. He tells him: *"Something is changing in the spiritual air. I can feel it. The Enemy is not asleep. Pray, Usagi. Pray for discernment."*

The player may not understand this yet. They will.

---

### Scene 3: The Market and the Warning

*[Gameplay: Exploration, NPC interaction, first hints of danger. Item shop tutorial.]*

The next morning, Usagi goes to the Nagasaki market with Kenji. The market is alive with commerce — Portuguese merchants selling clocks, Chinese traders with silk, Japanese potters, food stalls. The player can buy basic items (rice balls for healing, bamboo water flasks, a fishing line).

But there are also samurai in the market. More than usual. They wear the crest of **Lord Hasegawa Sahyoe**, the daimyo's enforcer. They are questioning merchants. Looking at faces. One of them carries a **fumi-e** — a bronze plaque bearing the image of Christ and the Virgin Mary.

The player overhears a conversation:

> **Samurai 1:** "Lord Hasegawa says the priests are still hiding in the villages. We are to find them."
> **Samurai 2:** "And the Christians?"
> **Samurai 1:** "They will be given the chance to step. Those who step are free. Those who do not..."

"To step" — **fumi-e wo fumu** — to trample the sacred image. The test that defined two and a half centuries of Christian persecution in Japan. If you stepped on Christ's face, you were free. If you hesitated — if your foot trembled — you were marked.

Kenji, who is not Christian, does not understand the weight of this. *"It's just a picture, Usagi. Just step on it and go home."*

Usagi says nothing. But the player sees his hands clench.

**Design Note:** This exchange establishes the game's central moral question early: *Is it ever acceptable to deny your faith to survive?* The game will ask this question in every single Book, across two thousand years, in increasingly devastating contexts. It never provides a simple answer. But it always shows the consequences.

## ACT I: THE GATHERING STORM

### Scene 4: The Night Visitors

*[Gameplay: Story-driven. Introduces the first moral choice.]*

Three days later. Night. Rain.

A pounding at Oba-chan's door. Usagi opens it to find **two Jesuit brothers** — young Japanese men, barely older than Usagi, terrified and soaking wet. They are **Brother Justino** and **Brother Miguel**, seminary students fleeing from Nagasaki where the authorities have begun arresting foreign missionaries. Father Tomas sent them here.

They need shelter. Hiding them is dangerous — if Lord Matsuda's men find harbored Christians, the entire village will be punished. But turning them away means sending them to capture and likely death.

**FIRST MORAL CHOICE:**

> **A:** *"Come in. You are safe here."* (Charity +1, Risk increases)
> **B:** *"I will take you to the cave. It is safer for everyone."* (Prudence +1, moderate risk)
> **C:** *"I... I can't. My grandmother..."* (No virtue change. The brothers leave into the rain. The player will meet them later — on crosses.)

This choice has consequences that ripple through the entire Book. Choice A raises the stakes for the village. Choice B is the wisest move. Choice C haunts Usagi — and the player — for the rest of the story.

---

### Scene 5: Kenji's Challenge

*[Gameplay: First combat tutorial.]*

The next day, Kenji drags Usagi to the bamboo grove behind the village for sword practice. Kenji has been training with his father's old katana; Usagi has never held a blade.

**Combat Tutorial:** Kenji teaches Usagi the basics — Slash, Guard, the timing window for counterattacks. The combat here is friendly sparring, low-stakes, but it introduces the FF-style turn-based system with a twist: **the Prayer Gauge**.

The Prayer Gauge builds during combat as Usagi takes damage or witnesses allies being hurt. When it fills, the player can access the PRAYER menu — healing, buffs, and eventually miracles. But early in the game, Usagi has almost no prayers unlocked. He is a farmer with a borrowed sword. The gap between what he is and what he will become is the entire character arc.

After the sparring, Kenji asks: *"If they come for your grandmother... will you fight?"*

Usagi: *"I don't know how to fight."*

Kenji: *"I will teach you. That is what friends are for."*

Kenji officially joins as the first **party companion**. He is the Warrior archetype — brave, capable, and faithless. His arc across Book Zero is learning that courage without a cause is just recklessness.

---

### Scene 6: Father Tomas's Story

*[Gameplay: Lore dump delivered as campfire conversation. Introduces the broader world.]*

That evening, Usagi visits Father Tomas in the cave. The priest is writing by candlelight — letters to the Jesuit Superior in Macau, pleading for guidance. He looks old. Tired.

He tells Usagi about the wider Christian world:

*"When I was young, in Lisbon, I heard about a great battle at sea — Lepanto, they called it. The Holy Father asked all Christendom to pray the Rosary, and God answered. The Turkish fleet was destroyed. I thought: God still acts in history. That is why I came to Japan. Because God acts through people who say yes."*

He pauses.

*"But sometimes the people who say yes are the ones who suffer the most."*

This is foreshadowing for the entire game. It is also the theological thesis: God's intervention in history is real, but it comes through human willingness to suffer, not through divine shortcuts.

Father Tomas gives Usagi a gift: a small devotional book — the **Doctrina Christiana**, the catechism translated into Japanese by the Jesuits. In gameplay terms, this is the player's first **Orashio codex entry** and unlocks the skill "Healing Touch."

---

### Scene 7: The Fumi-e

*[Gameplay: Stealth/evasion sequence. Major story beat.]*

Lord Matsuda arrives in Minamata with six samurai and a Buddhist temple official. The entire village is assembled in the town square. A fumi-e — a bronze plaque bearing the image of the Madonna and Child — is placed on the ground.

One by one, the villagers are called forward.

The Buddhist villagers step without hesitation. The nominal Christians — those who converted for trade advantages — step with visible discomfort but step. The genuine Kirishitan are last.

**Hana** is called. She looks at her father, the minor samurai, standing behind Lord Matsuda. She looks at the plaque. She steps. Her face is blank. She steps on Christ's face to protect her family. But the player can see — if they look carefully — that she is weeping.

**Oba-chan** is called.

She walks forward. She is tiny. Her back is bent from decades of farming. She looks at the plaque on the ground. She looks up at Lord Matsuda.

*"Deusu no ko wo fumu koto wa dekimasen."*
"I cannot step on the child of God."

Silence.

Lord Matsuda sighs. He does not want this. He gestures to his samurai.

**Oba-chan is arrested.** She is taken away. But as she passes Usagi, she presses something into his hand — the small **wooden cross** she has carried for thirty years. She whispers:

*"When the world is dark, hold this and pray. Heaven always answers — but not always the way you expect."*

This is the hinge of the entire game.

---

## ACT II: THE BREAKING

### Scene 8: The Rescue Attempt

*[Gameplay: First real combat sequence. Dungeon-style infiltration of the magistrate's compound.]*

Usagi and Kenji plan a desperate rescue. Father Tomas begs them not to — *"Violence will bring worse violence upon everyone"* — but Usagi cannot leave his grandmother to die.

The player infiltrates Lord Matsuda's compound at night. This is the game's first "dungeon" — a series of rooms with patrolling guards, locked doors (requiring keys found in side rooms), and traps. The combat encounters are against **Ashigaru** (foot soldiers) — basic enemies who fight with spears and are weak to flanking.

**Enemy Design — Ashigaru:**
- HP: 30 | ATK: 8 | DEF: 5 | SPD: 4
- Abilities: Spear Thrust (single target), Shield Wall (DEF +50% for 1 turn)
- Weakness: Holy damage, rear attacks
- These are not evil men. They are soldiers following orders. The game does not celebrate their deaths.

Deeper in the compound, Usagi encounters **Sergeant Ito**, a mid-boss — a veteran samurai who actually sympathizes with the Christians but serves his lord:

**Sergeant Ito:**
- HP: 120 | ATK: 15 | DEF: 12 | SPD: 8
- Abilities: Iaijutsu (fast draw, always goes first), Parry (negates one attack), Kiai Shout (lowers party ATK for 2 turns)
- Can be defeated in combat OR negotiated with if Usagi's Prudence is high enough

If the player talks to Ito instead of fighting (requiring they chose wisely in earlier scenes), Ito reveals: *"Your grandmother is in the lower cell. But they are moving her to Nagasaki at dawn. The magistrate has orders from Lord Hasegawa — she is to be made an example."*

---

### Scene 9: The Cell

*[Gameplay: Emotional cutscene with minimal player interaction.]*

Usagi reaches the cell. Oba-chan is kneeling on the dirt floor, praying the Rosary on her wooden beads. She is not afraid. She is radiant.

Usagi: *"Oba-chan, I have come to take you home."*

Magdalena: *"Usagi-kun. My home is not that little house anymore."*

She looks at him with perfect clarity:

*"I have prayed my whole life for one thing. Not for safety. Not for comfort. I prayed that God would send someone to help His people when the darkness comes. Not just here — everywhere. In every age. I prayed for a saint."*

She touches his face.

*"Heaven has answered me. You are the answer to my prayer."*

Usagi: *"I am nobody. I am a farmer. I can barely hold a sword."*

Magdalena: *"So was David. So was Peter. God does not call the qualified, Usagi. He qualifies the called."*

She presses the Rosary beads into his hand — alongside the wooden cross she already gave him.

*"Go now. Not because I am sending you away. Because He is sending you forward."*

Before Usagi can argue, the alarm sounds. Torches. Shouts. Soldiers are coming.

---

### Scene 10: The Martyrdom

*[Gameplay: Forced combat retreat. The player CANNOT win this fight.]*

Lord Hasegawa's elite samurai arrive — not Matsuda's reluctant soldiers, but professionals. **Hasegawa's Executioners** — four enemies at once, level-scaled to be unbeatable at this point in the game.

**Hasegawa's Executioners (x4):**
- HP: 200 | ATK: 25 | DEF: 18 | SPD: 12
- Abilities: Coordinated Strike (all four attack one target), Nodachi Cleave (hits entire party)
- The player is meant to lose this fight — or be forced to flee

Kenji is wounded. Usagi is overwhelmed. Father Tomas appears — he followed them — and interposes himself between the soldiers and Usagi.

*"Run, Usagi. NOW."*

Kenji grabs Usagi and drags him out of the compound. Behind them, they hear Father Tomas's voice, reciting the **Salve Regina** in Latin as the soldiers close in.

The player cannot go back. The game forces the retreat.

The next morning, from a hillside, Usagi sees three crosses erected on Nagasaki hill. Father Tomas. Brother Justino. And Oba-chan — tiny, crucified beside the priests, her lips still moving in prayer.

She is singing. The Orashio.

The game holds on her face for a long moment. Then silence.

**Virtue Change:**
- Faith is tested. If the player prays at a hidden shrine on the hillside (optional — the player must find it), Faith holds. If they do not, Faith drops by 1.
- Fortitude +1 (automatic — Usagi survived)
- Hope -1 (automatic — the loss is real)

## ACT III: THE CALLING

### Scene 11: The Wilderness

*[Gameplay: Open exploration. Grief mechanics. Side quests.]*

Usagi and the wounded Kenji flee into the mountains above Shimabara. The next sequence is deliberately quieter — the player wanders through cedar forests, tends Kenji's wounds, gathers food. There are no enemies for a time. The game breathes.

Usagi is in shock. The player can talk to Kenji at campfires, where the dialogue becomes deeply personal:

**Kenji:** *"I do not understand your God, Usagi. If He is real, why did He let her die?"*

**Usagi:** *"I do not know."*

**Kenji:** *"That is not good enough."*

**Usagi:** *"I know."*

There is no apologetics here. No tidy answer. Just two friends sitting with an unbearable question. This is where the game earns its theology — by refusing to shortcut grief.

During the wilderness section, the player can find:
- **Wild herbs** for crafting healing items
- A **mountain shrine** where an old Buddhist monk offers cryptic advice (and sells items)
- The ruins of an older Christian chapel — burned years ago — containing an **Orashio fragment** (unlocks "Guardian Angel" skill)
- **Hana**, who has fled the village after the fumi-e. She is ashamed. She stepped on the image. She feels she betrayed God. Usagi can tell her that God's mercy is bigger than her failure — or he can say nothing, and she leaves. If he speaks to her, she becomes an optional companion later.

---

### Scene 12: The Scream

*[Gameplay: Story trigger. Cannot be avoided.]*

On the third night in the mountains, Usagi cannot sleep. He sits alone by the dying fire, clutching Oba-chan's cross.

And he breaks.

Not quietly. Not nobly. He screams at the sky:

*"WHERE WERE YOU? She prayed every day of her life! She never doubted! She never complained! And You let them NAIL HER TO A CROSS! What kind of God does that? WHAT KIND OF GOD ANSWERS PRAYER WITH CRUCIFIXION?"*

He throws the cross into the darkness.

Silence. The fire crackles.

Then — light.

Not the soft golden light of a devotional painting. A light that splits the sky like a blade. White. Blinding. Terrible.

And in the light, a figure.

---

### Scene 13: The Messenger

*[Gameplay: Major cutscene. The game's cosmology is established.]*

**THE MESSENGER** stands in the clearing. Tall — impossibly tall, as if the concept of height is only loosely attached to him. His form is vaguely humanoid but shifting, like heat haze over a summer road. His voice is not a voice — it arrives in Usagi's mind fully formed, like a memory of something that has not happened yet.

The Messenger picks up Oba-chan's cross from where Usagi threw it. He holds it out.

**MESSENGER:** *"This was not discarded. It was planted."*

Usagi: *"Who are you?"*

**MESSENGER:** *"A carrier of messages. Your grandmother made a prayer with her last breath. Not for herself — she had already surrendered herself. She prayed for the world. For every age. For every place where the faithful cry out and no one comes. She asked God to send someone."*

Usagi: *"Send someone where?"*

**MESSENGER:** *"Everywhere. Everywhen. The faithful have always cried out, Usagi. In Rome, when Nero lit Christians as torches. In Egypt, when lies about the Son of God tore the Church apart. In Jerusalem, when holy war became unholy. In lands you have never seen, in centuries you cannot imagine. The cry is always the same: Help us. Send someone. We are alone."*

Usagi: *"And you want ME? I am a rice farmer. I could not even save my own grandmother."*

**MESSENGER:** *"You could not save her because she did not need saving. She is more alive now than you are. But others DO need saving — not from death, which comes for all, but from despair, which is the only true death."*

The Messenger extends two gifts:

**THE BLADE OF THE PILGRIM** — A katana that materializes from the light. Simple. Unadorned. Its steel has a faint luminous quality, as if moonlight were folded into the metal. It is not a holy weapon — not yet. It is a tool that will become holy through use, through sacrifice, through being wielded in defense of the helpless. Its stats are modest (ATK 12, SPD +2), but it grows with Usagi's Virtue score.

**THE CHARISM OF PASSAGE** — Not a physical gift but an alteration. The Messenger touches Usagi's chest and something *shifts*. Usagi feels the fabric of time become thin around him, like paper held up to a candle. He can almost see through it — almost see other places, other ages, other cries for help echoing across centuries.

*"You will not choose when or where. You will be called. When the faithful pray for intercession, when the need is desperate and the hour is dark — you will hear them, and you will go. This is not a power you control. It is a vocation you accept."*

Usagi: *"And if I say no?"*

**MESSENGER:** *"Then the cross remains in the dirt. And the prayers remain unanswered. And you live out your days as a farmer, and die, and are forgotten. And that would be a perfectly acceptable life. God does not force anyone to be a saint. He only asks."*

A long pause.

**PLAYER CHOICE:**

This is not a real choice — the game requires Usagi to accept (otherwise there is no game). But the WAY the player accepts matters:

> **A:** *"I will go. For Oba-chan."* (Hope +1 — motivated by love)
> **B:** *"I will go. Because someone has to."* (Justice +1 — motivated by duty)
> **C:** *"I will go. But I am still angry."* (Faith holds — honest anger is not faithlessness)

All three are valid. All three are honored by the narrative. The Messenger nods regardless:

*"Good. Anger is acceptable. Doubt is acceptable. Cowardice is not — and you are not a coward, Usagi. You are afraid, which is entirely different."*

---

### Scene 14: The First Passage

*[Gameplay: Kenji's farewell. First time-travel sequence.]*

Usagi returns to the campfire. Kenji is awake, staring at the blade in Usagi's hands.

**Kenji:** *"Where did you get that?"*

**Usagi:** *"I need to tell you something that will sound insane."*

He tells Kenji everything. Kenji is skeptical but loyal:

**Kenji:** *"So you are going to... travel through time? Fighting evil? Because your grandmother prayed?"*

**Usagi:** *"Yes."*

**Kenji:** *"...Can I come?"*

**Usagi:** *"I do not think it works that way."*

**Kenji:** *"Then who is going to watch your back?"*

This is the farewell. Kenji will stay in Japan. He will join the growing underground resistance. The player will meet him again — in Book V, "The Hidden Church," set decades later, when Usagi returns to Japan and discovers what became of his friend.

*[Hint for players who pay attention: Kenji's descendants will be among the kakure kirishitan of the Goto Islands.]*

The air around Usagi begins to shimmer. The Charism of Passage activates — not through his will, but through a distant, desperate prayer reaching across seventeen centuries. He can hear it: a voice in a language he does not know, crying out from beneath a great city:

*"Domine, miserere nobis... Domine, adiuva nos..."*
(Lord, have mercy on us... Lord, help us...)

The portal opens. Rome, 64 AD.

Usagi looks at Kenji one last time.

**Kenji:** *"Come back."*

**Usagi:** *"I will try."*

He steps through.

**END OF BOOK ZERO.**

## EMOTIONAL DESIGN NOTES

### What Book Zero Must Accomplish

1. **Make the player love Oba-chan in 20 minutes.** If the player does not care about the grandmother, the entire game falls apart. She must be specific — her mannerisms, her laugh, the way she scolds Usagi, the rice cakes she makes. She is not a plot device. She is a person. Her death must HURT.

2. **Establish that this game does not pull punches.** The grandmother dies. The priest dies. The village is shattered. The player cannot prevent any of this. This signals: this game respects you enough to show you real consequences.

3. **Make the calling feel earned, not cheap.** Usagi does not get powers because he is special. He gets them because his grandmother prayed for sixty years and he said yes. The power is not his — it is grace, flowing through him because he was willing to be a vessel.

4. **Introduce the fumi-e as the game's recurring motif.** Every Book will present its own version of the fumi-e — the moment where a character is asked to choose between safety and faith. Always painful. Never simple.

5. **Leave threads for every future Book.** Kenji's fate. Hana's shame. The kakure kirishitan survival. The Messenger's true nature. The Accuser's first whisper (Lord Matsuda's "reasonable" compliance is the Accuser's first face — though the player will not realize this until Book III or IV).
